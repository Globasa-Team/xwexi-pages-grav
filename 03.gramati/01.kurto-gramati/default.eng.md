<strong><em>Bonata!</em></strong> ("Welcome!") This page presents a brief but comprehensive reference grammar for the international language <strong>Globasa</strong>.

<em>Note:</em> When a section of text is colored <span style="color: gray;">grey</span>, that means that part is optional and is usually left out.

### Stress

Vowels are <em>a</em>, <em>e</em>, <em>i, o, u</em>. Consonants are all other letters: <em>b</em>, <em>d</em>, <em>y</em>, <em>w, etc.</em>

- The stress is placed on the last vowel when a word ends in a consonant:

    <strong>jab<u>a</u>l</strong> mountain

    <strong>kaw<u>a</u>y</strong> cute

- The stress is placed on the second-to-last vowel when a word ends in a vowel:

    <strong>est<u>u</u>di</strong> study

    <strong>kr<u>o</u>a</strong> frog

    <strong>D<u>u</u>nya</strong> Earth 

- Monosyllabic content words (<strong>xey</strong>, <strong>glu</strong>, <strong>yam</strong>, etc) are stressed.

- Monosyllabic prepositions (<strong>per</strong>, <strong>tas</strong>, etc), conjunctions (<strong>ji</strong>, <strong>mas</strong>, etc) and particles (including verb particles) are unstressed.

Pronouns are used at the end of a noun phrase with implicit nouns.

- <strong>Dua <em>to</em> sen maxmo bon kom un <em>to</em>.</strong> Two are better than one.

## Nouns and verbs

### Nouns

There is no singular–plural distinction.

- <strong>uma</strong> horse, horses

There are no <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Article_(grammar)">articles</a>.

- <strong>mahi</strong> a fish, the fish

Most nouns do not inherently indicate gender.

- <strong>gami</strong> wife, husband, spouse

- <strong>kuku</strong> hen, rooster, chicken

In a sequence of two nouns, the second noun identifies the first. This is known as <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apposition">apposition</a>.

- <strong>Bahari Pacifiko</strong> Pacific Ocean

- <strong>Xaher Neoyork</strong> New York City

- <strong>misu sodar Alis</strong> my sister Alice

Abstract nouns can be formed from concrete nouns, adjectives and adverbs by using the suffix <strong>-ya</strong>.

- <strong>atreya</strong> parenthood (<strong>atre</strong> parent)

- <strong>hoxya</strong> happiness (<strong>hox</strong> happy)

### Noun–verbs

Nouns and verbs in Globasa share the same form.

- <strong>Te <em>lala</em> bon <em>lala</em>.</strong> S/he <em>sings</em> a good <em>song</em>.

- <strong>Mi <em>yam</em> tiga <em>yam</em> fe moy din.</strong> I <em>eat</em> three <em>meals</em> every day.

The main copula is <strong>sen</strong> (<em>to be</em>).

- <strong>Sola sen brilapul.</strong> The Sun is bright.

Globasa has three auxiliary verbs: <strong>abil</strong> (<em>can</em>), <strong>ingay</strong> (<em>should</em>) and <strong>musi</strong> (<em>have to, must</em>). 

- <strong>Mi abil na doxo.</strong> I can read.

- <strong>Mi ingay na doxo.</strong> I should read.

- <strong>Mi musi na doxo.</strong> I have to read. / I must read.

## Adjectives and adverbs

Adjectives and adverbs in Globasa share the same form. 

- <strong>bon</strong> good, well

- <strong>asan</strong> easy, easily

When an adjective/adverb word is used before a noun or pronoun, it functions as an adjective.

- <strong><u>kijawi</u> gras</strong> <u>green</u> grass

In all other cases, the word is used as an adverb.

Adverbs have more flexibility in terms of position in the sentence.

- <strong>Ete <u>hox</u> swikara teyan.</strong> They <u>happily</u> accept the offer. (Adverb placed before a verb.)

- <strong>Ete swikara teyan <u>hox</u>.</strong> They accept the offer <u>happily</u>. (Adverb placed at the end of the clause.)

- <strong><u>Hox</u>, ete swikara teyan.</strong> <u>With joy</u>, they accept the offer. (At the start of the sentence, a comma separates the adverb from the subject. Without the comma, <strong>hox</strong> would have been an adjective in the example sentence.)

The suffix <strong>-li</strong> is used to turn a noun into an adjective or adverb.

- <strong>syensi<u>li</u> metode</strong> scientific method (<strong>syensi</strong> science)

- <strong>na digita<u>li</u> penci</strong> to digitally edit (<strong>digita</strong> digit)

The suffix <strong>-mo</strong> is added to an adjective/adverb word that is modifying another adjective/adverb word<strong>.</strong>

- <strong>syahe<u>mo</u> roso</strong> dark red

- <strong>na godo<u>mo</u> velosi pala</strong> to speak too quickly

Globasa supports <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adjective_phrase">adjective phrases</a>, which follow the noun.

- <strong>basa <u>palado fal mega insan</u></strong> the language <u>spoken by a million people</u>

- <strong>fleytora <u>maxmo velosi kom soti</u></strong> the airplane <u>faster than sound</u>

Emphasis over the strength of a quality can be expressed with <strong>daydenmo</strong>. It's similar to <em>So</em>.

- <strong>Daydenmo yukwe!</strong> How pleasant! "So pleasant!"

- <strong>Daydenmo gao drevo!</strong> What a tall tree! "A so tall tree!"

### Prepositional phrases

Prepositional phrases that modify the entire sentence have freedom to move anywhere in the said sentence. Before the verb, they require commas.

<table class="wikitable" style="text-align: center;">
  <tr>
    <td><strong>Mi oko teve <u>in ogar</u>.</strong><br /><strong>Mi oko <u>in ogar</u> teve.</strong><br /><strong>Mi, <u>in ogar</u>, oko teve.</strong><br /><strong><u>In ogar</u>, mi oko teve.</strong></td>
    <td>I watch TV at home.</td>
  </tr>
</table>

### Indirect object

The preposition <strong>tas</strong> (<em>to/for</em>) marks the indirect object.

- <strong>Yu le gibe yawxe <u>tas</u> mi.</strong> You gave the keys to me.

- <strong>Yu le gibe <u>tas</u> mi yawxe.</strong> You gave me the keys.

- <strong><u>Tas</u> mi yu le gibe yawxe.</strong> To me, you gave the keys.
